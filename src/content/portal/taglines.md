# Fast Web Guru — Tagline Shortlist
*Created: 2026-06-07*
*Sources: website-copy-v2.md, product-marketing.md, customer-research.md, copy-pillars.md*

---

## Scoring Criteria

Each tagline is scored 1–5 on three dimensions:

- **Clarity** — Does a non-technical Australian SMB owner understand it in under 3 seconds?
- **Differentiation** — Does it describe something no competitor says?
- **Memorability** — Would it stick in someone's head after one read?

**Max score: 15**

---

## Direction 1: Speed / Done-For-You

*The unclaimed territory: "done for you, today" is not said by any profiled competitor. This direction leads on the primary VOC pain (waiting weeks for simple changes) and the primary emotional trigger (betrayal — I did everything right and got left behind).*

---

### Tagline 1.1 — The Frontrunner
**"Your website. Done for you. Today."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 5 | 5 | **15/15** |

**What it does well:** Three-beat rhythm. Each beat builds on the last. "Your" signals ownership and personalisation. "Done for you" names the category and the benefit simultaneously. "Today" is the killer word — it's the answer to the most common VOC phrase ("I've been waiting two weeks for a five-minute update"). No competitor says any of this.

**Where to use it:** Primary website tagline. Below the logo. Hero headline or subheadline. Social bio.

**Weakness:** None found. This is the benchmark.

---

### Tagline 1.2
**"Website sorted. Today."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 4 | 4 | **13/15** |

**What it does well:** "Sorted" is the most resonant single-word outcome in the Australian SMB register — the ideal end state described in their own language. Punchy. No corporate words.

**Where to use it:** Social posts. Ad copy. Text over an image. Slightly more casual than 1.1.

**Weakness:** Less specific about the "done for you" part — could imply DIY. Better as a supporting tagline than a hero.

---

### Tagline 1.3
**"We fix it before you notice."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 4 | 5 | 5 | **14/15** |

**What it does well:** Directly answers the highest-value VOC desire: "I don't have to worry about it — they even contact me when they find a problem." Implies proactive support. Suggests a team that's always watching. Slightly mysterious — earns a second read.

**Where to use it:** Support/reliability section. Homepage third scroll. Email subject lines. Social ads.

**Weakness:** Doesn't communicate the done-for-you breadth — sounds like monitoring only. Use as a secondary line, not the primary.

---

### Tagline 1.4
**"Done. No invoice. No chase. Today."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 5 | 4 | **14/15** |

**What it does well:** Stacks three pain point resolutions in four words before the payoff. "No invoice" addresses the invoice-reflex trigger directly. "No chase" names the disappearing developer problem. "Today" lands the speed promise.

**Where to use it:** Pricing page. Homepage problem section. Email subject lines. Facebook ads for cold audiences who've experienced this.

**Weakness:** Four beats is one beat too many for a primary tagline. Strong as a subheadline or in-body pull quote.

## Direction 2: Access / Enterprise AI

*The AI angle: 88% of Australian SMBs using AI report stronger revenue growth, but most can't access the stack. FWG's no-gatekeeping model — same AI from $40 as at $1,000+/mo elsewhere — is genuinely unusual.*

---

### Tagline 2.1
**"Enterprise AI. SMB price."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 5 | 5 | **15/15** |

**What it does well:** The shortest possible form of the access story. The contrast between "enterprise" and "SMB" does all the work — no feature list needed. Reads like a bold claim that earns investigation.

**Where to use it:** Ads (especially Google search, where character count is limited). Hero tagline alternative. Email subject lines. Pricing page intro.

**Weakness:** "Enterprise" might read as slightly corporate for the tradie register. For tradie-heavy audiences, 2.2 may perform better.

---

### Tagline 2.2
**"The AI stack big businesses pay $1,000/month for. Yours from $40."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 5 | 3 | **13/15** |

**What it does well:** Specific anchoring — "$1,000/month" gives a reference price, "$40" is the contrast. This works as a pricing-page headline or a value proof statement. The specificity makes it credible, not salesy.

**Where to use it:** Pricing page hero. Mid-page value statement on homepage. Not a hero tagline — too long.

**Weakness:** Too long for a tagline. Best as a headline on a specific page.

---

### Tagline 2.3
**"Same AI. Fraction of the price."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 4 | 4 | 4 | **12/15** |

**What it does well:** Short, clean, accessible. Works without the price specifics when those aren't relevant (social bio, print, swag).

**Where to use it:** Social bios. Swag. Partner/advocate materials. Email footers.

**Weakness:** "Fraction of the price" is slightly vague — the specific "$40" version is stronger. Use when you can't include the price context.

## Direction 3: Partnership / Team

*The human-in-the-loop angle: "one person who knows your site" is a top VOC desire — but the word "team" resonates for breadth. The tension between personal (one person) and capable (team) is the brand's sweet spot.*

---

### Tagline 3.1
**"Your digital team, from $40/month."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 4 | 4 | **13/15** |

**What it does well:** "Your digital team" is inclusive and expansive — covers hosting, updates, AI, support. The price anchor immediately repositions what "team" can cost. Strong for an audience that wants the breadth of an agency but can't afford one.

**Where to use it:** About page. Partnership page. Email intros. Service comparison sections.

**Weakness:** Less differentiated from managed service competitors than Direction 1 or 2. "Digital team" sounds like many agencies. Better as a supporting line.

---

### Tagline 3.2
**"One person. One price. Your whole website sorted."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 5 | 5 | **15/15** |

**What it does well:** "One person. One number. One fixed price." is a direct quote from VOC research — the ideal service relationship described by real prospects. This tagline mirrors that language exactly. Each "one" reduces a different kind of complexity.

**Where to use it:** Homepage after the hero. How It Works page header. Email subject lines. Strong for warm audiences who've already read the problem section.

**Weakness:** Third read is needed to catch all three beats — can lose the "sorted" payoff in fast scanning. Best used where the reader has a second to pause (not an ad, not a billboard).

---

### Tagline 3.3
**"The website team you couldn't afford — until now."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 4 | 5 | **14/15** |

**What it does well:** Classic "status reversal" frame — names the frustration (this is out of reach) and resolves it in one beat. The dash creates a natural pause before the payoff. Conversational rhythm.

**Where to use it:** Homepage mid-section. Ad copy. Email re-engagement campaigns for prospects who didn't convert.

**Weakness:** "Until now" is a familiar copywriting construct — may read as slightly formulaic for readers who consume a lot of marketing. Test against 3.2 for this audience.

## Direction 4: Simplicity

*The "just works" angle: the top outcome phrase across every VOC source is "it just works." This direction names that aspiration directly — no jargon, no complexity, just business on.*

---

### Tagline 4.1
**"Website done. Business on."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 4 | 5 | **14/15** |

**What it does well:** Two-beat parallel structure. "Done" closes the website chapter; "Business on" names what that unlocks. Rhythm is natural and punchy — lands on the beat. The implied message: your website is no longer something you deal with. It's dealt.

**Where to use it:** After the hero as a secondary tagline. Email sign-offs. Social posts. Print and event materials.

**Weakness:** "Business on" could be read as "business operating" rather than "get back to running your business." Ensure surrounding context clarifies the meaning on first use.

---

### Tagline 4.2
**"One fixed price. No surprises. No chasing."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 5 | 5 | 3 | **13/15** |

**What it does well:** Directly addresses the top three VOC pain points in order: invoice shock, hidden fees, developer disappearance. Each phrase is an objection resolved. Strong for cold audiences who pattern-match immediately to a bad experience.

**Where to use it:** Pricing page. Below a CTA button. Email subject lines. Facebook ad body copy.

**Weakness:** A list of negatives — tells you what's absent, not what's present. Use alongside a positive frame (e.g. "Your website. Done for you. Today. // One fixed price. No surprises. No chasing.").

---

### Tagline 4.3
**"Less chasing. More business."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 4 | 3 | 4 | **11/15** |

**What it does well:** Paired contrast works. Rhythm is clean. "Chasing" is the right word — it's exactly how SMBs describe their experience with web developers.

**Where to use it:** Social posts. Email subject lines. Subheadlines.

**Weakness:** Could describe a dozen services (CRM, project management, etc.). Lowest differentiation score of the set. Use when you can't include the FWG-specific context.

---

### Tagline 4.4
**"Your website. Their problem now."**

| Clarity | Differentiation | Memorability | Total |
|---------|----------------|-------------|-------|
| 4 | 5 | 5 | **14/15** |

**What it does well:** Unexpected framing — reframes the transfer of ownership in a way that feels like a relief. "Their problem now" is conversational, slightly cheeky, and lands the "done for you" message via contrast rather than direct statement. Memorable because it subverts expectations.

**Where to use it:** Ad copy for retargeting (people who've visited but not converted). Email subject lines. Social posts. Not a hero tagline — better as a punchline.

**Weakness:** "Their problem now" could be read as careless ("they've handed it off to someone who doesn't care"). Context matters — needs a warm intro before it lands right.

## Recommendation

**Primary tagline:** "Your website. Done for you. Today."
— Covers all three core messages (ownership, service model, speed), uses no corporate language, lands the key differentiator ("today"), and has no meaningful competitor equivalent.

**Pairing line (for pages where primary tagline appears):** "Enterprise AI. SMB price." or "One fixed price. No surprises. No chasing."
— The primary tagline answers "what"; the pairing line answers "how much" or "is it trustworthy."

**A/B test to run first:** "Your website. Done for you. Today." vs "One person. One price. Your whole website sorted." — the second is grounded in exact VOC language, which may outperform on warm audiences.
